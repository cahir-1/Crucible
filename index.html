The Crucible and it's connection to McCarthyism

What happened at the beginning?

The beginning of The Crucible follows a group of girls who go into the forest to dance. Innocent, supposed to be fun. Then, it follows with accusations of witchcraft, backstabbery, and later ostracization and imprisonment. But, prior to all of this, Salem had to be established. Salem was established as a religious community, fearing that the bordering land was the land of the devil. This relates to the American mind of communism where the Soviet Union, China, and Eastern Europe were all of communist parties, therefore evil. Once people come in and out of these places, though, people become suspicious if they had encountered the idea of evil they curated in their minds, suspecting even their closest friends or lovers. Why did it happen?

This goes along with the last sentence stated in the previous section, but it is good to elaborate on. Basically, all of the chaos can be summed up to humans being scared. You are scared of the unknown, of the evil, and if someone were associated with that presence you'd be scared of them too. In the crucible, the hysteria is brought about by dancing in the forest, which sparks rumors of witchcraft. Abigail says on page 6, line 41, "Uncle, we did dance; let you tell them l confessed it— and I’ll be whipped if I must be. But they’re speakin’ of witchcraft. Betty’s not witched." This is a direct objection by someone at the forefront of the event causing confusion, but the damage was already done. The people of the village wouldn't take that for an answer as Betty's illness is highly unusual, causing the girls involved with the dancing to lie their way out of persecution. This relates to McCarthy as those accused as a communist were forced to either out others to save themselves, or face a punishment they didn't necessarily deserve.

How did it escalate?

Both events escelated due to fear of ostracization, which was understandable as anyone outed as a witch or communist would lose their civil rights, along with the possibility of being executed. In the Crucible, the story progresses and hysteria grows as none of the original suspects are willing to state what really had happened, and instead opted to make lies in hopes of drawing attention away from themselves. During the Red Scare, Joseph McCarthy accused a lot of people himself. It wasn't as expansive as in the Crucible, but due to his proposal of freedom for convicted communists to out others, he essentially pulled the strings of mass hysteria, rather than a group causing it.

How did it resolve?

Finally, the Crucible resolves with Proctor's confession and execution and an end to the mass hysteria. He dies regretful, but a good man. The Red Scare ended with McCarthy's followers losing faith in him, and with that he lost his power, dying soon after. Both are similar due to the fact that it shows how once embodiment of hysteria has lost its head it cannot continue any longer.

The Takeaway

The main thing that we can take away from these incidents and stories is to always trust our own judgement. Both of these events, fiction or not, are brought about by people buying into a mob mentality. The Crucible is caused by young women lying and making false judgement claims. The Red Scares ruined the lives of many people all because of one man's suspicion. If we can use our own judgement to determine and deem what we think is worthy information or not we can lead as stronger people, not bound by a single persons idea. We're responsible for what we believe is true, and that also means we stand up to what we believe isn't true. This is honestly just a pretentious way of dragging out the idea of 'think for yourself,' but I do believe the importance of that idea still stands today. We have so man outlets of media, so many credible and uncredible sources, that it is a necessity to be able to identify and determine what false information is. The Salem Witch Trials began around four hundred years ago, and the red scare only happened about half a century ago. It doesn't matter what time period we live in, what matters is what we make of our situation, and how strong our conviction to our morals is.
